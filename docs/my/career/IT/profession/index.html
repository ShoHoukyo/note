https://www.quora.com/Is-software-development-really-a-dead-end-job-after-35-40 learn, grow, and gain skills that are valuable That can mean learning new technologies, but it also means learning people skills, management, public speaking, sales, operations, accounting, finance, or any other thing that would make you more valuable to any company. At many companies, there is no viable "technical ladder" for software developers: a title like "senior engineer" is the highest available to software developers, and after this pay grade maxes out software developers have to go into management roles -- whether they're suited to them or not -- in order to advance. "Management" isn't just about managing people. At a company with a future someone has to manage technology -- the CTO, for example. Many CTOs still code, and few would call CTO a dead-end job. Here's where that different future comes in: the challenge for a very experienced developer is to find companies that value the perspective provided by experience. So, the available jobs are more scarce and provide less variety for very experienced people.

value to the org (IBM though) Someone who can connect people together to fit an array of projects, who understands different infrastructures and can quickly get things done within a large system, who has areas of deep knowledge and experience, and who can come up with new standards, who can strategize about business cases, and come up with new concepts to patent and can act as a bit of an evangelist, et cetera.

I would think a question like this will suffer from Quora response bias. That is: Quora has an extremely high percentage of programmers relative to the overall public. A high percentage of them are successful. Successful developers (especially in later career) are more likely to answer with enthusiasm than ones who dead-ended with depression.

Another approach that may work for people not cut out to be a manager but still wishing to advance is to acquire business domain knowledge and choose a business niche. For myself, while I bounced around industries at the start of my career, I found an interest in quant finance and later studied for my MBA to improve knowledge in both math and finance, as well as a handful of other business related skills.

While my current position requires a solid background in software development in multiple languages (currently: Java, C#, C++, R, python, Excel/VBA), it also requires elements of statistical modelling, financial products, technical writing and project management. I don't have any full direct reports, but when I am asked to lead a project I get to pick a couple suitable junior analysts or interns from a pool to help out.

In summary, I partially agree that by 35-40 a software developer should attempt to acquire more skills than simply writing code. Acquiring people management skills is simply one route, but there are other choices as well.

the management track: yeah, but…businesses need an order of magnitude fewer software managers than they need worker-bees. And senior management provides another order-of-magnitude fewer chairs, plus it’s a different skill set to development, though of course some make the transition. entrepreneurship: this is the most typical route from developer to senior manager. But only 1% of startups become profitable, so this doesn’t account for many jobs. consulting: this is a common route. Devs become consultants for a variety of reasons. Some are very good and have developed valuable specialized skills. Some aren’t very good, and have trouble landing full-time jobs. The stakes are lower to hire a consultant. You get rid of them if they don’t perform. Some build a business out of a code library. Some are good at cold-calling and selling, but these guys were never really devs in the first place. early retirement: Developers who have been fully employed for 30 years and have lived frugally may have enough money to retire. It gets harder and harder to find work when you’re over 40 because you want more money, and because age discrimination is a big thing in software development. Eventually people reach a point where it isn’t worth the effort any longer.
