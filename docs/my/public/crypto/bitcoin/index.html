Correct. A Bitcoin private key is a 256-bit number, which is often represented as a 64-character hexadecimal string.

Now, let's explore the incredibly slim chance of randomly generating a private key that corresponds to an existing public address.

The Mind-Bogglingly Small Probabilityâ€‹

To calculate the probability of finding a private key for any existing public address on a single try, we need two numbers:

  1. The total number of possible private keys.
  2. The number of public addresses that are currently in use (i.e., have a non-zero balance).

Hereâ€™s the breakdown:

  • Total Possible Private Keys: A private key is a 256-bit number, so the total number of possibilities is $2^{256}$. This is an astronomically large number, approximately $1.1579 \times 10^{77}$. To put that in perspective, the estimated number of atoms in the observable universe is around $10^{80}$.

  • Active Public Addresses: The number of Bitcoin addresses with a non-zero balance fluctuates. For our calculation, we'll use a generous estimate of 50 million active addresses.

The calculation is straightforward:

$$\text{Probability} = \frac{\text{Number of Active Addresses}}{\text{Total Possible Private Keys}}$$

$$\text{Probability} = \frac{50,000,000}{2^{256}}$$

This gives us a probability of approximately:

$$4.318 \times 10^{-70}$$

This number is so infinitesimally small that it's difficult to comprehend. For all practical purposes, the probability is zero.

Putting it in Perspectiveâ€‹

To give you an idea of how unlikely this is, consider the following:

  • The odds of winning the Powerball lottery jackpot are about 1 in 292 million. Randomly generating a used private key is unimaginably less likely.
  • It is more likely that you would win the Powerball jackpot several times in a row than to randomly generate a private key that is already in use.

In short, the security of Bitcoin relies on the sheer vastness of the private key space. It is computationally infeasible to guess or randomly generate a private key that someone else is using.

Even if you used extraordinarily fast hardware for an entire year, the chance of randomly generating a private key that matches an existing, funded address is still, for all practical purposes, zero.

Let's break down the calculation to see why.

The Ultimate Guessing Machineâ€‹

Instead of just "fast hardware," let's imagine you could control a machine with the combined power of the entire Bitcoin network. This is the most powerful SHA-256 hashing system on the planet.

  • Total Network Hashrate: The Bitcoin network's power is immense, currently around 600 exahashes per second (EH/s).
    • 1 exahash = 1 quintillion ($10^{18}$) hashes.
    • So, this machine makes $600,000,000,000,000,000,000$ guesses every second.

The Calculationâ€‹

Now, let's see how many keys this ultimate machine could generate in one year and what its chances of success would be.

  1. Total Guesses in One Year:

    • Seconds in a year: $365 \text{ days} \times 24 \text{ hours/day} \times 60 \text{ minutes/hour} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute} \approx 31.5 \text{ million}$ seconds.
    • Total guesses: $(600 \times 10^{18} \text{ guesses/sec}) \times (31.5 \times 10^6 \text{ sec/year}) \approx 1.89 \times 10^{28}$ guesses per year.

  2. Probability of a "Hit": We use the same formula as before, but now we multiply the single-try probability by the total number of guesses in a year.

    • Chance per guess: $4.318 \times 10^{-70}$ (from our previous calculation)
    • Total Chance in One Year: $(1.89 \times 10^{28} \text{ guesses}) \times (4.318 \times 10^{-70} \text{ chance/guess})$

This gives us a total probability of approximately:

$$8.16 \times 10^{-42}$$

Your chance of success, even using the combined power of the entire global Bitcoin network for a full year, is a number with 41 zeroes after the decimal point before you get to a non-zero digit.

To put this into perspective, you have a better chance of:

  • Winning the Powerball lottery jackpot six times in a row.
  • Picking one specific, predetermined atom from the planet Earth.

The 256-bit security of Bitcoin's private keys creates a number space so vast that it is secure against brute-force attacks, not just for a human lifetime, but for timescales that defy human imagination.
